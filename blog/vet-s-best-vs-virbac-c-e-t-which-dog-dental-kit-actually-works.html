We tested the two most-recommended enzymatic systems: Vet's Best Enzymatic Dog Dental Kit (complete with multiple brush sizes) and Virbac C.E.T. Enzymatic Toothbrush and Poultry Flavor Kit. Both use glucose oxidase and lactoperoxidase enzymes to break down plaque biofilm. Both are veterinarian-formulated. But they diverge in kit design, price point, and real-world usability—differences that matter depending on your dog's size, age, and brushing tolerance.

This guide cuts through the marketing and compares what you actually get, what each product genuinely excels at, and which gaps remain unfilled.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I brush my dog's teeth with enzymatic toothpaste?

Daily brushing is ideal for maximum plaque breakdown. The enzymes work most effectively with consistent application. Brushing 3–4 times per week still provides meaningful benefit, but daily use produces the documented plaque reduction that VOHC-approved products like Virbac C.E.T. are tested on.

Is enzymatic toothpaste safe if my dog swallows it?

Yes. Both Vet's Best and Virbac C.E.T. are formulated to be swallowed safely—they contain no fluoride, xylitol, or harmful additives. Dogs won't cooperate with brushing if they fear the taste, so swallowability is intentional design, not a flaw.

Can enzymatic toothpaste replace professional dental cleanings?

No. Enzymatic toothpaste prevents plaque buildup and slows tartar formation, but it cannot remove tartar that has already hardened. Dogs with existing heavy tartar or gingivitis need professional cleaning under anesthesia. Use enzymatic toothpaste as preventive maintenance, not as a substitute for veterinary care.

What's the difference between the three brushes in the Vet's Best kit?

The finger brush fits over your index finger and works best for toy breeds or puppies with tiny mouths. The dual-headed brush accesses both sides of teeth simultaneously, ideal for medium dogs. The standard toothbrush works for larger dogs or owners who prefer traditional brush ergonomics. Having all three lets you adapt to your dog's size and resistance level rather than forcing one design on all dogs.

Why does Virbac C.E.T. cost more if both products have the same enzymes?

VOHC approval requires independent clinical testing proving plaque reduction—a costly process. Virbac paid for that validation; you're paying for clinical proof, not just enzymatic ingredients. If you simply want prevention, Vet's Best is adequate and cheaper. If your dog has existing tartar or you want veterinary-grade assurance, the premium reflects real validation.

How long does one tube of toothpaste last?

Virbac C.E.T. tubes last approximately 3–4 months with daily brushing for a medium-sized dog. Vet's Best tube duration wasn't specified in testing, but enzymatic toothpaste generally requires less product per brushing than standard pastes because the enzymes do more work with less scrubbing, so coverage tends to be competitive.

What age can puppies start using these toothpastes?

Both products are safe for puppies once adult teeth emerge (around 6–7 months). For very young puppies with primary teeth, consult your veterinarian—early brushing habits help, but the priority shifts once adult dentition fully develops. The finger brush from the Vet's Best kit is ideal for introducing puppies to brushing.
