Pros

Cons

Our Verdict

At $25–$45, the Catit Digger earns its place if you have a moderately intelligent, moderately food-driven cat. The 4.3-star rating reflects real satisfaction among owners whose cats actually engaged with it. Where it falls short is capacity and the assumption that your cat will instantly understand the concept. Buy this if you're specifically trying to slow down a fast eater or enrich an indoor cat's afternoon. Skip it if you need a high-volume feeder or if your cat has already lost interest in puzzle toys. It's not the cheapest option, but it's not the most expensive either—and the build quality justifies the mid-range pricing.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Catit Digger compare to the Northmate similar-priced alternatives?

Northmate models typically cost $5–$10 more but offer larger capacity. The Digger's advantage is the adjustable difficulty and the leg-engaging mechanism. Northmate is better if raw volume matters; Catit is better if enrichment depth matters. Both hover around 4.2–4.4 stars, so you're choosing philosophy, not quality.

Will this work for dry food, or only wet?

Exclusively dry kibble. The pockets are shallow and designed for standard cat kibble to sit in shallow grooves. Wet food will just pool at the bottom and get messy. Some owners have tried mixing, and it creates a consistency problem that defeats the purpose.

How long does a cat usually take to solve it once they understand it?

Depends entirely on adjustment level and your cat's experience. On easiest setting, 3–5 minutes. On hardest setting, 10–20 minutes for a practiced cat. This is why the adjustable difficulty matters—it keeps the challenge fresh across weeks of use, preventing the 'solved it once, ignored it forever' problem that kills cheaper feeders.
