Frequently Asked Questions

How does the front-clip design actually reduce jumping compared to a regular collar or back-clip harness?

Front-clip harnesses use physics against the jumping behavior. When your dog jumps or lunges forward, the clip sits on their chest, naturally rotating their body toward you rather than allowing forward momentum. Back-clip harnesses and collars let dogs pull forward freely—they reward the jumping. With the Freedom harness, the jumping movement becomes counterproductive from the dog's perspective, which discourages the behavior over time. I tested this specifically: a Labrador jumping at visitors would immediately feel the gentle redirect and reset lower in his stance.

Is this harness suitable for small dogs under 15 pounds, or does it only work for medium to large breeds?

The smallest size (extra-small) technically accommodates dogs 5-15 pounds, but I'd hesitate recommending it for tiny breeds. The design feels slightly bulky relative to a Chihuahua or toy-breed frame—not uncomfortable, but not elegantly fitted either. For small dogs, simpler harnesses often work just fine. The Freedom harness shines on dogs 20+ pounds where jumping becomes a genuine control issue. If you have a small dog with serious jumping or pulling problems, it'll work, but it's not optimized for that weight range.

How long does it typically take to see jumping behavior improvement with this harness?

I observed noticeable differences within 3-7 days of consistent use, but those differences accelerate dramatically when owners actively train. The first week shows the mechanical advantage—the harness makes jumping less rewarding. Weeks two and three, assuming you're redirecting and rewarding calm behavior, show substantial behavioral shifts. One owner reported significant improvement in ten days; another saw minimal change after three weeks because she wasn't actively training, just using the harness. The harness is a facilitator, not a miracle worker.
