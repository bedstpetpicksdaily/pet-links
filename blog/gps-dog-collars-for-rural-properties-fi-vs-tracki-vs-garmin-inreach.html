GPS Dog Collars for Rural Properties: Fi vs. Tracki vs. Garmin inReach

⏱11 min read
By Best Pet Picks Daily • July 21, 2026 • Contains affiliate links
Affiliate Disclosure: As an Amazon Associate, we earn from qualifying purchases. Clicking our links costs you nothing extra and helps us keep reviewing products for free.

Rural property owners face a genuine problem that suburban dog owners rarely encounter: you can't visually monitor a dog roaming across 5+ acres, especially when they're bred to work independently. A standard leash doesn't work on working dogs, and Bluetooth collars become useless beyond a few hundred feet—leaving you vulnerable to losing your dog miles from home.

The challenge is finding a GPS collar that actually works in remote terrain where cell towers are sparse, battery life extends beyond 2-3 days, and the device survives mud, water, and rough terrain. Not all GPS collars are built for this environment. Some rely on cellular coverage that simply doesn't exist in dead zones. Others drain batteries so quickly that constant recharging becomes impractical for a working farm dog.

This guide compares three category-leading options that handle large rural properties, livestock guardian dogs, herding breeds, and hunting dogs: the Fi Smart Dog Collar, Tracki 2.0 GPS Tracker, and Garmin inReach Collar. Each excels in different scenarios, and choosing wrong means either constant recharging, losing signal in critical moments, or overpaying for features you don't need.

Fi Smart Dog Collar

The Fi uses cellular GPS (LTE) with up to 20 days of battery life in standard mode, with no separate phone subscription required beyond the device itself. It offers real-time location updates, customizable geofence alerts, and a Rest Mode feature that extends battery life on low-activity days. The lightweight design won't fatigue a dog's neck over long hours of work, and the app includes activity tracking alongside location history.

Battery longevity is the Fi's standout advantage—20 days of battery life means farm dogs that stay within 5-10 miles of home can operate for weeks between charges. However, Fi relies on cellular coverage, making it less suitable for properties in true dead zones where cell towers are completely absent.

Best for: Farm dogs on managed rural properties with at least intermittent cell coverage; owners who want the longest battery life and don't need satellite fallback.

Tracki 2.0 GPS Tracker

Tracki 2.0 combines cellular and GPS tracking with more aggressive real-time updates than Fi, offering faster location refresh rates for actively roaming dogs. It's designed for both active dogs and general pet tracking, with weatherproof (though not fully rugged) construction suitable for outdoor use.

The device balances real-time accuracy with reasonable battery life, though it typically requires more frequent charging than the Fi—usually every 5-7 days depending on activity level. Tracki works well in mixed coverage areas where you have intermittent cell service but need faster positional updates than a 20-day battery model can provide.

Best for: Active dogs on properties with variable cell coverage; owners who prioritize faster location updates over maximum battery life; those needing mid-range pricing.

Garmin inReach Collar

Garmin inReach stands apart by using satellite communication (Iridium network) rather than relying solely on cellular towers. This makes it the only option that functions in true dead zones—areas where no cell service exists. It provides global coverage and works in remote terrain where competitors lose signal entirely.

The tradeoff is cost and battery life. Garmin inReach typically requires more frequent charging than Fi and involves higher subscription fees for satellite connectivity. However, for properties in genuinely remote areas or those spanning vast distances where cell coverage is non-existent, it's the only viable option that guarantees tracking capability.

Best for: Remote rural properties with no cellular coverage; hunting dogs ranging across wilderness; livestock guardian dogs on large acreage in mountainous or heavily forested terrain.

  • ✓ Garmin inReach uses satellite technology to track dogs in true dead zones where cellular collars fail completely
  • ✓ Tracki 2.0 offers faster real-time location updates suitable for actively roaming working dogs
  • ✓ All three options provide geofencing and activity tracking alongside location history for comprehensive monitoring
Cons
  • ✗ Fi and Tracki rely on cellular coverage and become unreliable or non-functional in areas without cell towers
  • ✗ Garmin inReach carries higher subscription costs and shorter battery life compared to cellular-only alternatives

Our Verdict

Choose Fi Smart Dog Collar if your rural property has at least intermittent cell service and you want the longest interval between charges. The 20-day battery life is genuinely exceptional for farm dogs that operate within established ranges, and the lack of mandatory subscription fees makes it the most economical long-term choice for cellular coverage areas.

Choose Tracki 2.0 if you need faster real-time location updates than Fi provides and your property has variable cellular coverage. It strikes a middle ground between battery life and update frequency, making it suitable for dogs that roam more actively but still within areas with reasonable cell service.

Choose Garmin inReach if your property is in a genuine dead zone or spans wilderness where no cellular service exists. Satellite coverage is non-negotiable for remote hunting properties, mountainous terrain, or extensive acreage where cellular collars simply won't function. The higher cost and subscription fees are unavoidable tradeoffs for reliability in areas where competitors cannot operate.

Before purchasing, verify the coverage map for your specific area—GPS accuracy and signal strength vary dramatically in remote locations with dense tree cover or mountainous terrain. Check battery life claims against real-world usage reviews, as active dogs may drain batteries faster than manufacturer specifications suggest.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why can't I just use a Bluetooth collar for my dog on a large rural property?

Bluetooth collars work only within a few hundred feet of the receiver—typically 100-300 feet depending on interference. On a 5+ acre property, your dog can easily wander beyond that range and disappear. GPS collars use either cellular networks or satellites to track your dog across miles, not just your immediate vicinity.

Do these collars work if my property has no cell towers nearby?

Fi and Tracki rely on cellular networks and will have reduced or no functionality in dead zones. Garmin inReach uses satellite technology and works in true dead zones, but at higher subscription cost. If you're certain you have zero cellular coverage, Garmin is the only viable option.

How often do I need to charge these collars?

Fi Smart Collar lasts up to 20 days in standard mode. Tracki 2.0 typically requires charging every 5-7 days with active use. Garmin inReach generally needs more frequent charging due to satellite communication demands. Active dogs may drain all collars faster than manufacturer specs suggest.

Can I use these collars for off-leash training?

Yes, all three are designed for off-leash monitoring. However, none should be considered a replacement for proper training or recall commands. They're backup safety tools in case a dog wanders beyond controlled boundaries.

Which collar is most durable for working dogs?

All three are waterproof or water-resistant, but durability depends on your specific terrain. Fi is lightweight and won't fatigue a dog's neck during long work hours. Garmin inReach is built for extreme conditions but is bulkier. Tracki offers moderate durability. Check reviews for your exact use case (mud, rocky terrain, water work).

Is there a monthly subscription cost?

Fi Smart Collar has no required monthly subscription—you pay once for the device. Tracki 2.0 typically includes a subscription. Garmin inReach requires satellite subscription fees (more expensive than cellular). Factor subscription costs into your long-term budget.
