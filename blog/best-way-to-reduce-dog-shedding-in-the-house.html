The best way to reduce dog shedding in the house is through regular brushing, a high-quality diet, and maintaining proper hydration. Combining these three factors addresses shedding at its source while significantly reducing the amount of loose hair your dog leaves around your home.

The Short Answer

Reducing dog shedding starts with brushing your dog 3-5 times per week using a deshedding tool designed to remove loose undercoat before it falls out. Equally important is feeding your dog high-quality food rich in omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids, which strengthen hair follicles and promote healthier skin. Ensure your dog drinks plenty of water and gets regular exercise, as dehydration and stress can increase shedding. While you can't stop shedding completely, these methods can reduce it by 50-80% depending on your dog's breed.

The Full Explanation

Dog shedding is a natural biological process, and most dogs shed year-round to some degree. However, excessive loose hair in your home is preventable through a multi-faceted approach.

Brushing is your first line of defense. The undercoat—the soft, dense layer beneath your dog's outer coat—is where most shedding originates. When you brush regularly, you physically remove loose hair before it falls onto your furniture and floors. The frequency depends on your dog's breed: double-coated breeds like Golden Retrievers and German Shepherds need daily brushing during shedding season, while single-coated breeds may need 2-3 times per week. Brushing also improves circulation, distributes natural oils, and allows you to catch skin problems early.

Nutrition plays a critical role in coat health. Dogs fed low-quality kibble often have dry, brittle coats that shed excessively. Look for dog foods that list real meat as the first ingredient and contain omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids. These essential fatty acids reduce inflammation, strengthen skin barrier function, and promote healthier hair growth. Some dogs benefit from fish oil supplements, which can noticeably improve coat quality within 6-8 weeks.

Hydration and stress management matter more than most owners realize. Dehydrated dogs develop dry skin, which leads to increased shedding. Ensure fresh water is always available and consider adding moisture-rich foods like wet food or bone broth to meals. Additionally, stress increases cortisol levels, which triggers shedding. Regular exercise, consistent routines, and a calm environment help reduce stress-related hair loss.

Bathing can also help, but with caution. Bathing your dog once a month (or every 6-8 weeks for some breeds) with a moisturizing shampoo can help remove loose hair and dead skin cells. However, over-bathing strips natural oils and worsens shedding. Use lukewarm water and quality dog shampoo—never human shampoo, which alters the pH of dog skin.
