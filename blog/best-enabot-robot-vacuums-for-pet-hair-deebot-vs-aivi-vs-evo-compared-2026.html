Enabot's lineup spans from the mid-range Aivi C Supreme ($400–$600 range) to the flagship Deebot X2 Omni ($1,200+), with the Evo Max and older Deebot X1 Omni sitting in between. Each claims to handle pet hair differently—some with dedicated AI detection, others with self-emptying stations, a few with mopping capabilities. The question isn't which one is "best"; it's which one solves your specific pet hair problem without wasting money on features you'll never use.

After reviewing the real-world testing data and specs across these models, here's what actually separates them.

Enabot Deebot X2 Omni

The flagship model at $1,200+. Features auto-mopping with mop-lifting mechanism, self-emptying station (empties every 2+ weeks), lidar mapping for multi-room navigation, and dedicated pet hair detection using AI to adjust suction automatically. The self-cleaning dock and combo vacuum/mop approach targets homes where pet accidents and shedding are both serious concerns.

Best for: Pet owners with multiple rooms, multi-level homes, and frequent messes (accidents + shedding). Worth it if you want a complete cleanup system and have budget for premium.

Enabot Deebot X1 Omni

The previous-generation flagship at roughly $900–$1,000. Offers similar auto-mopping and self-emptying capabilities to the X2, with mop-lifting for carpet transitions. Tested extensively with golden retrievers and cats; handles strategic hairball placement and post-grooming chaos. Navigation works in complex layouts; maintains accuracy on first run.

Best for: Pet owners who want flagship features at a slightly lower price point than X2. Good if you need mopping + vacuuming in one and don't need the absolute latest tech iteration.

Enabot Aivi C Supreme

Mid-range option ($400–$600 range) with lidar mapping and pet detection via sensors, but without self-emptying or mopping. Creates accurate floor plans on first run; avoids getting stuck under couches or bumping walls randomly. Works best in open spaces; may struggle with complex layouts or multiple room transitions. Dustbin capacity is smaller, requiring more frequent manual emptying.

Best for: Budget-conscious pet owners with simpler floor plans, single-story homes, or lighter shedding. Good entry point if you want lidar navigation without premium pricing.

Enabot Evo Max

Mid-range performer with genuine pet hair avoidance sensors that detect and route around hair clumps before they cause tangles—a key differentiator from cheaper models. Dustbin capacity of 220ml+. No self-emptying or mopping. Handles post-grooming chaos and daily shedding without constant intervention. Consistent 4.3-star ratings across 500+ reviews.

Best for: Pet owners wanting pet-specific features (avoidance sensors) without self-emptying complexity. Best for those with moderate shedding in open-to-mid-complexity homes.

Enabot Evo Nano

Entry-level option designed for pet hair but with fewer premium features. Smaller form factor, more affordable price point. Details on specs are limited in source material, but positioned as budget-friendly alternative for basic pet hair cleanup without advanced mapping or mopping.

Best for: Budget-first pet owners or those with smaller living spaces who want automatic cleaning without spending $600+.

Our Verdict

The Deebot X2 Omni wins overall if budget allows—the self-emptying station and mopping capability solve two major pet owner pain points (hair + accidents), and the 2+ week emptying cycle is genuinely valuable for households with multiple shedders. However, it's not the only smart choice.

The Deebot X1 Omni delivers 90% of X2's functionality at 20–25% lower cost, making it the smart upgrade path if you want auto-mopping and self-emptying without cutting-edge features.

The Evo Max is the sweet spot for pet owners who don't need mopping: it includes genuine pet hair avoidance sensors (unlike basic models), consistent performance across 500+ reviews, and costs $300–$400 less than mid-tier self-emptying models. Best value for pure suction performance.

The Aivi C Supreme makes sense only if your home has simple layouts and you're willing to manually empty the dustbin regularly. Lidar mapping is a real advantage, but the smaller capacity makes it tedious for households with serious shedding.

The Evo Nano is a last resort for true budget constraints or very small spaces—you're trading convenience for price.

Real question: Do you need mopping (X1/X2) or just suction (Evo Max)? Does your home layout match lidar navigation capabilities? How often will you tolerate manual maintenance? Answer those three, and the right model becomes obvious.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often do self-emptying stations need maintenance, and can they handle heavy pet hair buildup?

Self-emptying docks (X1/X2 Omni) require filter cleaning every 2–4 weeks and dust bag replacement roughly every 30 days in heavy-shedding households. The dock itself needs periodic wiping to prevent blockages, especially with pet hair. They're convenient, but not completely maintenance-free.

Does pet hair detection actually work differently from regular motion sensors?

Yes—dedicated pet hair detection (on X2 and Evo Max) uses AI or advanced sensors to identify fur patterns specifically and adjust suction in real-time, while basic models rely on simple obstacle avoidance. The difference is whether the vacuum actively hunts pet hair or just reacts when it bumps into clumps.

Which model should I choose if I have multiple pets and a multi-level home?

The Deebot X2 Omni is designed for multi-room, multi-level layouts with its advanced mapping. However, robot vacuums can't handle stairs themselves, so you'll need separate units per floor or manual intervention. If budget is tight, the X1 Omni handles multi-room layouts similarly at lower cost.

Is the mopping feature on X1/X2 Omni worth the extra cost over a vacuum-only model?

It depends on your secondary need. If pet accidents are common (puppies, senior pets), mopping adds real value. If shedding is your only concern, you're paying $300–$500 for a feature you might use rarely—the Evo Max offers better pure suction value in that case.

How does dustbin capacity affect real-world performance with multiple shedding pets?

Models with 220ml+ capacity (Evo Max, self-emptying docks) need emptying/replacement every 1–2 weeks in multi-pet households. Smaller bins (Aivi C Supreme) require 2–3 times weekly emptying, making automation feel pointless. If you have two or more shedding pets, self-emptying or 220ml+ is almost mandatory.

Do these models actually get stuck under couches or bump into walls like cheaper vacuums?

Lidar-mapped models (X1/X2, Aivi C Supreme) avoid the wall-bumping and random behavior of budget models. However, all models can get stuck under low furniture or between tight obstacles. Lidar is a real advantage, but home layout still matters—simple, open spaces work best across all models.
