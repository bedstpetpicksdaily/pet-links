Natural vs. Regular Cat Treats: A Real Comparison for Pet Owners in 2026

By Best Pet Picks Daily • July 20, 2026 • Contains affiliate links

The cat treat aisle has become increasingly complex. Pet owners now face a genuine choice between mass-produced regular treats and premium natural alternatives—and the decision isn't as straightforward as marketing suggests. Both categories have legitimate trade-offs in cost, ingredient quality, palatability, and actual nutritional impact.

This guide compares what's actually inside natural versus regular cat treats, examines specific brand approaches (including PetTreatery), and helps you understand whether the premium price tag translates to real health benefits for your cat. We'll also cover what to watch for on ingredient labels, since the term "natural" isn't regulated by the FDA for pet treats—meaning savvy shopping matters.

Regular Commercial Cat Treats

Mass-produced treats found in most grocery stores and pet chains. These typically contain artificial flavors, colors, and preservatives like BHA or BHT to extend shelf life. Primary ingredients are often meat by-products rather than whole meat sources, combined with grains and fillers designed to maximize profit margins.

Convenient, widely available, and inexpensive—often 50–70% cheaper per ounce than natural alternatives. However, the ingredient list frequently includes synthetic additives and recognizable fillers that offer minimal nutritional value beyond calories.

Best for: Budget-conscious owners, cats with no digestive sensitivities, or as occasional training rewards where cost is the primary concern.

Natural Cat Treats (Premium Brands like PetTreatery)

Formulated with minimal processing and whole, recognizable ingredients such as chicken, fish, turkey, and vegetables—no artificial flavors, colors, or synthetic preservatives. Preservation relies on safer techniques like freezing or natural methods rather than chemical additives. Brands like PetTreatery specifically prioritize ingredient transparency and source high-quality, pronounceable components.

Higher nutritional density per treat, typically with elevated protein content and wholesome supplements like fish oil, pumpkin, or sweet potato. These treats avoid fillers and by-products, meaning your cat receives genuine nutrition rather than empty calories. Price per ounce is notably higher (often 2–3× regular treats), but portion control may offset costs since natural treats typically satisfy cats more effectively.

Best for: Owners prioritizing ingredient quality, cats with known allergies or digestive sensitivities, and those seeking long-term health optimization over short-term savings.

Mid-Range Hybrid Treats

A middle ground between commercial and premium natural options. These treats may use real meat as primary ingredients but still contain some artificial preservatives or fillers. They're easier to find than fully natural brands and cost 30–50% less than premium options while offering ingredient quality above typical grocery-store treats.

Useful for owners who want better ingredients without committing to premium pricing. However, they still contain some synthetic additives—just fewer than fully commercial options—so they don't eliminate concerns about artificial preservatives entirely.

Best for: Owners seeking moderate ingredient quality at a reasonable price point, as a stepping stone before fully transitioning to premium natural treats.

Grain-Free or Allergy-Specific Natural Treats

A subset of natural treat brands that remove common allergens (grains, fish, poultry) or focus on single-protein sources. These appeal to owners whose cats have documented sensitivities or allergies. They maintain the ingredient transparency of premium natural treats while eliminating specific triggers.

More specialized and often more expensive than general natural treats. Only necessary if your cat has diagnosed allergies or sensitivities; feeding grain-free without a medical reason offers no documented health advantage.

Best for: Cats with confirmed food allergies, sensitivities to specific proteins, or digestive issues that improve with simplified ingredient lists.

Our Verdict

Regular commercial treats remain appropriate if: Your cat has no sensitivities, thrives on current food without digestive issues, and you're using treats primarily for training rewards or occasional enrichment. Frequently Asked Questions

Are natural cat treats worth the extra cost?

It depends on your cat's needs and your budget. For cats with allergies, sensitivities, or digestive issues, the ingredient quality may deliver measurable health improvements that justify the premium. For cats thriving on regular treats without sensitivities, the health benefit is unproven. Consider natural treats worth testing (not necessarily worth the long-term cost) unless your cat shows specific dietary triggers.

Can switching to natural treats cause digestive upset?

Yes, if introduced too quickly. Any significant dietary change can trigger temporary digestive changes. Introduce natural treats gradually over 7–10 days, mixing them with your cat's current treats. If upset persists beyond that window, your cat may have a sensitivity to specific ingredients—not to the "natural" category itself.

How often can I give my cat treats (natural or regular)?

Treats should comprise no more than 10% of your cat's daily caloric intake. For most cats, this translates to 2–5 small treats daily, depending on treat size and calorie density. Natural treats are often more calorie-dense, so you may give fewer per day while your cat remains satisfied.

What ingredients should I avoid in cat treats?

Avoid treats with artificial preservatives (BHA, BHT, ethoxyquin), artificial colors or flavors, and excessive fillers. Check that the first ingredient is recognizable meat (chicken, fish, turkey), not meat by-products or bone meal. If your cat has known allergies, read labels carefully—even "natural" brands can contain common allergens.

Does 'natural' automatically mean safer or healthier?

No. The FDA doesn't regulate the term "natural" for pet treats, so brands can use it loosely. A "natural" treat might still contain fillers or unnecessary ingredients. Always read the full ingredient list and look for third-party certifications (like AAFCO standards compliance) rather than relying on marketing labels alone.

Can natural treats help with my cat's allergies?

If your cat's allergies are food-related, switching to natural treats with simpler, allergy-free ingredient lists can help. However, not all natural treats are hypoallergenic—you'll need to choose brands offering grain-free or single-protein options that avoid your cat's specific triggers. Consult your vet to confirm food allergies before investing in premium alternatives.

How should I store natural cat treats?

Natural treats lack chemical preservatives, so they spoil faster than regular treats. Store in an airtight container in a cool, dry place, or refrigerate/freeze to extend shelf life. Check expiration dates and discard treats showing signs of mold or unusual odor. Regular commercial treats have longer shelf lives due to synthetic preservatives.
