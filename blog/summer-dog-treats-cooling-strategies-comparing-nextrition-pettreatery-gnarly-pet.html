Summer Dog Treats & Cooling Strategies: Comparing Nextrition, PetTreatery & Gnarly Pet (2026)

Summer heat poses genuine challenges for dog owners: overheating risk, digestive sensitivity to cold treats, and the need for high-quality nutrition during active months. Rather than settle for the first "cooling treat" brand you find, it's worth understanding what actually differentiates the major options available in 2026.

This guide compares three established premium brands—Nextrition Pet, PetTreatery, and Gnarly Pet—alongside DIY frozen treat strategies and gear-based cooling solutions. Each approach has legitimate strengths and real limitations depending on your dog's age, activity level, digestive sensitivity, and how you plan to use treats (training rewards, enrichment, or pure cooling relief).

When comparing cooling treat brands, check the ingredient list for natural freezing agents like water or fruit puree rather than artificial additives, and verify the package size matches your pet's needs since treats meant for multiple uses can melt quickly once opened in summer heat.

Editor's Tip

If you're buying blindly, you'll likely overspend or choose treats that don't match your dog's actual needs. This comparison cuts through marketing language to show you what each option genuinely delivers.

Nextrition Pet: Gut-Healthy Cold-Pressed & Natural Formulations

Nextrition Pet specializes in cold-pressed nutrition and gut-healthy formulations specifically designed for warm-weather digestion. Their approach emphasizes premium natural ingredients with no artificial preservatives, colors, or sweeteners. The brand positions itself around digestive health, making them particularly relevant for dogs with sensitive stomachs that react poorly to frozen or cold treats consumed too quickly.

Their product range includes both traditional treats and cold-pressed food options, which can serve double duty as both nutrition and cooling relief. Nextrition's marketing emphasizes that many commercial dog treats upset digestion with artificial ingredients and excessive sugars, leaving dogs sluggish in heat—a real concern for dogs with pre-existing digestive issues.

Best for: Dogs with sensitive stomachs; owners prioritizing digestive health over novelty; dogs needing nutritionally-dense, ingredient-conscious options year-round

PetTreatery: Frozen Premium Snacks with Natural Cooling Ingredients

PetTreatery has positioned itself as a leader in premium frozen dog treats, offering natural options with cooling ingredients like pumpkin and watermelon that provide hydration alongside nutrition. Their collection emphasizes indulgence paired with nutrition, and they specifically design products for summer cooling rather than year-round digestive support.

The brand offers flexibility across dog life stages (puppies and senior dogs) and caters to dogs suitable for training use during summer activities. PetTreatery's strength is in the frozen treat category itself—their products are engineered as relief tools rather than nutritional staples, meaning they work best as supplements to a primary diet rather than meal replacements.

Best for: Dogs who enjoy frozen treats; training-focused owners needing high-value summer rewards; dogs without digestive sensitivities; supplemental cooling relief

Gnarly Pet: Protein-Packed Chews for Active Dogs

Gnarly Pet differentiates by targeting active, working dogs specifically. Their natural treats and protein-packed chews are formulated to support peak performance during warm months, making them distinct from cooling-focused brands. Gnarly Pet emphasizes freeze-dried or low-moisture options that maintain nutritional value while being safe to store without refrigeration during outdoor activities—a practical advantage for hiking, training, or beach days.

The brand's approach combines summer training treats with activity-based enrichment rather than purely cooling solutions. This makes them valuable for owners managing both heat safety and behavioral stimulation, though less ideal for dogs needing immediate cooling relief or with digestive concerns triggered by protein-heavy chews.

Best for: Active dogs; owners doing summer training or outdoor activities; dogs needing long-lasting enrichment; freeze-dried treat preference

DIY Frozen Treats (Homemade Pumpkin, Watermelon, Broth-Based)

Creating frozen treats at home—using natural ingredients like pumpkin puree, watermelon, or low-sodium bone broth—offers complete control over ingredients and eliminates packaging waste. Homemade frozen treats cost significantly less per serving than premium brands and allow customization for dogs with specific allergies or sensitivities.

The trade-off is time and consistency. Homemade treats won't have the shelf-stable convenience of commercial options and require advance planning. You also lose the nutritional engineering of brands like Nextrition, which have formulated their recipes for digestive support—homemade watermelon ice may cool your dog but won't support gut health.

Best for: Budget-conscious owners; dogs with multiple food allergies; owners comfortable with basic food prep; dogs needing simple hydration more than nutrition

Cooling Gear (Vests, Mats) + Standard Premium Treats

Rather than relying solely on treat-based cooling, combining cooling vests or mats with any of the above treat brands addresses summer safety more comprehensively. Cooling gear requires proper fit—measure your dog and verify dimensions before purchasing, and check reviews specifically from owners with your dog's size/breed to confirm whether products run true to size.

This approach decouples cooling (handled by gear) from nutrition and training rewards (handled by treats), allowing you to choose treats based on training needs or digestive health rather than cooling properties. It's the most practical for dogs doing summer activities, since physical cooling tools don't require consumption and work alongside any treat choice.

Best for: Active dogs doing outdoor training; owners combining multiple cooling strategies; dogs sensitive to frozen treats; comprehensive heat-safety approach

Our Verdict

The honest comparison: There's no single "best" choice because summer dog care isn't monolithic. Your actual pick depends on what problem you're solving.

Choose Nextrition Pet if: Your dog has a history of digestive upset, especially around frozen treats or heat-related GI sensitivity. The gut-health focus justifies the premium price for dogs where digestive support prevents bigger problems. Cold-pressed options also serve as meal supplementation, not just treats.

Choose PetTreatery if: You want premium frozen treats specifically engineered for cooling and your dog tolerates them without digestive issues. This brand excels at the frozen-treat category itself—if that's what you need, they've optimized for it. Less ideal for dogs with sensitivities or owners doing intense summer training.

Choose Gnarly Pet if: Your dog is active and you're combining summer training with heat management. The freeze-dried format's outdoor stability matters for hiking or beach days where refrigeration isn't available. Skip this if your dog has digestive sensitivities triggered by high protein.

DIY frozen treats make sense if: Budget is the constraint and your dog has no specific digestive needs. Watermelon and pumpkin ice work for basic cooling and hydration. Combine with cooling gear for comprehensive safety without premium brand markup.

Start with cooling gear (vest/mat) regardless of treat choice. It addresses overheating independently and lets you pick treats based on training, nutrition, or digestive health rather than forcing cooling functionality into your treat selection. Many dogs benefit from both cooling management (gear) and strategic nutrition (treats) working in parallel, rather than expecting treats alone to solve summer heat stress.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which brand is safest for dogs with sensitive stomachs in summer?

Nextrition Pet's gut-healthy formulation is specifically designed for digestive sensitivity. Avoid PetTreatery frozen treats and high-protein Gnarly Pet chews if your dog has heat-triggered digestive issues. Simple DIY frozen options (plain watermelon ice) are also safe, but lack the digestive support of Nextrition's engineered formulas.

Can I use any of these treats for summer training, or only specific brands?

PetTreatery and Gnarly Pet are both marketed for training use. Nextrition treats work for training but emphasize nutrition over high-value reward properties. DIY frozen treats are low-value for training (most dogs prefer higher-interest options). For serious summer training where treats are motivation, PetTreatery or Gnarly Pet are better bets.

What's the actual difference between 'cooling treats' and regular premium treats?

Cooling treats (PetTreatery) are frozen format with hydrating ingredients like watermelon. Regular premium treats (Gnarly Pet) are shelf-stable, nutrient-dense, and support activity. Nextrition blurs the line with cold-pressed formats. The distinction matters: frozen treats physically cool mouth and throat; premium nutrient-dense treats support performance but don't cool. Cooling gear does what neither accomplish.

Should I refrigerate Gnarly Pet treats during summer, or does freeze-dried stability matter in practice?

Gnarly Pet's freeze-dried/low-moisture format is legitimately stable without refrigeration during outdoor activities—a real advantage if you're doing multi-hour training or hiking. However, at home, you can refrigerate any treats. The advantage is only meaningful if you're regularly away from cooling access during outdoor activities.

How do I know if my dog is actually overheating, versus just being hot?

Signs of overheating include excessive panting, drooling, lethargy, disorientation, or collapse. A hot dog pants normally and rests in shade. If you see concerning signs, get your dog to cool water and shade immediately, and contact a vet. Treats alone won't prevent heat stress—shade, hydration, limited exercise during peak heat, and (in severe cases) cooling gear are primary safety tools.

Are frozen treats actually bad for dogs, or is that just marketing concern?

Some dogs with digestive sensitivities do react poorly to frozen treats consumed too quickly, especially in heat—that's a real issue Nextrition acknowledges. Most healthy dogs tolerate frozen treats fine. Watch your individual dog for signs of GI upset (vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy) and adjust accordingly. For sensitive dogs, room-temperature premium treats (like Gnarly Pet) are safer than frozen options.

Do I really need 'summer-specific' treats, or can I use my dog's regular treats?

You don't strictly need summer-specific treats. Regular premium treats work year-round. However, summer-specific options add hydration (PetTreatery's watermelon), support active performance (Gnarly Pet's protein), or prioritize digestive health in heat (Nextrition). If your dog does fine on regular treats, stick with them. If heat triggers digestive issues or you want added cooling relief, summer-optimized options justify consideration.

What's the most cost-effective approach for summer cooling?

DIY frozen treats (watermelon, pumpkin) are cheapest per serving. Store-brand premium treats (if your dog tolerates them) cost less than Nextrition, PetTreatery, or Gnarly Pet. However, 'cost-effective' changes if your dog has digestive sensitivities requiring Nextrition, or if you're training intensively and need high-value rewards. Pairing mid-range treats with a cooling mat/vest is often more practical than relying on premium treats alone.
