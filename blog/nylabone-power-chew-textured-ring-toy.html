Frequently Asked Questions

How does this actually compare to the original Nylabone Power Chew?

The textured ring is designed specifically for grip and texture variety, while the original power chew (usually a stick or bone shape) targets different chewing angles. The ring's advantage is that it can't be swallowed whole and the hollow center distributes pressure differently. For my heavy chewer, the ring lasted about the same length of time, but felt safer because of the design. If your dog likes variety, rotating between different Nylabone shapes keeps them interested longer.

Will this work for puppies or only adult dogs?

Use caution with puppies under 6 months. Their baby teeth can't handle the density, and they'll shred it faster (sometimes into small pieces). The Nylabone website recommends the Power Chew line for dogs over 6 months with permanent teeth, but honestly, wait until 9+ months if your puppy is an aggressive chewer. For adult dogs of any age with healthy teeth, this works great.

Is it really better than Kong or Bully Stick alternatives?

Different tools for different jobs. Kongs are better for treat-stuffing and mental enrichment (especially important in summer when you need distraction). Bully sticks are higher-value rewards but dissolve completely, leaving nothing behind. The Nylabone Power Chew Ring sits between these—it's pure chewing, no treats, no mess, and it stays around for weeks. If your main goal is keeping your dog occupied without constant replacement, this wins. If you want enrichment puzzles or occasional high-value treats, combine it with other options.

Does it actually survive a dog who swallows toys?

If your dog actively tries to swallow toys whole, this is actually one of your best options because the rigid ring shape and hollow center make it nearly impossible to compress into a swallowable size. However, if your dog is a serious swallower, supervise anyway and consider consulting your vet about anxiety-related behavior. The ring's design is safer than rope toys or smaller chew toys, but it's not a replacement for proper supervision or behavioral training.
