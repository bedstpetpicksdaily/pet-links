What Is the Dog Sunscreen Spray?

Dog sunscreen spray is a specially formulated sun protection product designed to be safe for your dog's skin and coat. Unlike human sunscreen — which can contain zinc oxide and other ingredients that are toxic to pets — pet-safe sunscreen sprays are made with dog-friendly formulas that block harmful UV rays without putting your furry friend at risk. They typically come in an easy-to-apply spray bottle so you can cover large areas quickly, even on a wiggly pup who doesn't exactly love standing still.

Most quality formulas offer broad-spectrum protection (UVA and UVB), are fragrance-free to avoid irritating sensitive noses, and are water-resistant for outdoor adventures. They're designed for the areas most vulnerable to sun damage — the nose, ears, belly, and any spot where the fur is thin or sparse. Think of it as the doggy version of your beach bag essential, just way less likely to get sand in it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use human sunscreen on my dog?

No, you should never use human sunscreen on your dog, as many formulas contain zinc oxide and para-aminobenzoic acid (PABA), which are toxic to dogs if licked or absorbed through the skin. Always use a sunscreen spray specifically formulated and labeled as safe for dogs. If your dog accidentally ingests human sunscreen, contact your veterinarian or the Pet Poison Helpline immediately.

What SPF sunscreen should I use on my dog?

Veterinarians generally recommend using a dog-safe sunscreen spray with an SPF of 30 or higher for adequate sun protection. Dogs with light-colored coats, pink skin, or thin fur are especially vulnerable and may benefit from SPF 50. Reapply every 4-6 hours, or more frequently if your dog has been swimming or playing in water.

Where do you apply sunscreen spray on a dog?

Focus on applying dog sunscreen spray to the most sun-exposed and vulnerable areas, including the nose, ear tips, belly, groin, and any patches of thin or light-colored fur. These areas have less pigment and hair coverage, making them most susceptible to sunburn and long-term skin damage. Avoid spraying directly into your dog's eyes, mouth, or open wounds.

How do I know if my dog got sunburned?

Signs of sunburn in dogs include red, pink, or inflamed skin, especially on the nose, ears, and belly, along with dry or flaky skin that your dog may scratch or lick excessively. In severe cases, you may notice blistering, hair loss, or your dog showing signs of pain when the area is touched. If you suspect a serious sunburn, consult your veterinarian, as repeated sun damage can increase the risk of skin cancer in dogs.
