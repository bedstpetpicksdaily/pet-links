Frequently Asked Questions

What do cats need for enrichment at home?

Cats need a combination of physical, mental, and sensory stimulation including climbing structures like cat trees, scratching posts, interactive toys, puzzle feeders, and window perches to watch outdoor activity. Providing hiding spots, tunnels, and rotating toys regularly prevents boredom and reduces stress-related behaviors. A complete setup should also include opportunities for play that mimic hunting, such as wand toys and motion-activated toys.

How do I set up an enriching environment for an indoor cat?

Start by creating vertical space with cat trees, wall-mounted shelves, or cat walkways so your cat can climb, perch, and survey their territory from different heights. Add a window bird feeder outside a sunny window, scatter puzzle feeders throughout the home, and dedicate at least two 10-15 minute interactive play sessions daily. Rotating toys every few days keeps the environment feeling fresh and mentally stimulating.

How much enrichment does a cat need per day?

Most cats benefit from at least 20-30 minutes of active, intentional enrichment daily, split into two or more shorter sessions to match their natural hunting rhythm. This should include interactive play with a human using wand or feather toys, plus independent enrichment like puzzle feeders or window watching throughout the day. Under-stimulated cats often develop problematic behaviors like excessive meowing, destructive scratching, or overeating.

What are the best enrichment toys for cats?

The most effective cat enrichment toys include wand toys with feathers or crinkle attachments for interactive play, treat-dispensing puzzle feeders like the LickiMat or Kong Wobbler, and battery-operated motion toys for solo play. Crinkle balls, catnip-filled kickers for bunny kicking, and cardboard boxes or paper bags provide low-cost sensory enrichment many cats love. The best toys are ones that trigger your cat's prey drive by moving unpredictably or engaging their sense of smell.
