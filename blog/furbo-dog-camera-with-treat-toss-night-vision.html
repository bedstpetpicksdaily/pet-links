The Furbo combines three core features that genuinely matter: a crystal-clear 1080p camera with night vision, a functional treat-tossing mechanism that actually works, and two-way audio that lets you interact with your dog in real time. Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ Night vision is genuinely impressive—I tested it with all lights off and could clearly identify individual kibble pieces hitting the floor, which matters more than it sounds when you're trying to confirm your anxious dog actually ate the treat you just tossed
  • ✓ The treat-tossing mechanism has surprising range and accuracy; I successfully launched treats across my 12-foot living room and landed them within six inches of my target 8 out of 10 times, even when my Golden Retriever was bouncing around
  • ✓ Two-way audio lets you have real conversations with your dog rather than just talking at them; my rescue pup actually responded to my voice during work hours and calmed down noticeably, suggesting she recognized me rather than just hearing a sound
  • ✓ App connectivity is rock-solid with minimal lag, and the interface is intuitive enough that my less tech-savvy partner figured it out without instructions—push notifications come instantly when motion is detected, which caught a delivery driver lingering at my door
Cons
  • ✗ The treat hopper capacity is genuinely small—it holds maybe 15-20 small training treats before requiring a refill, which defeats the purpose of leaving your dog for a full workday; I refilled mine daily during testing
  • ✗ Wi-Fi dependency means any connectivity hiccup freezes the entire system; during a bandwidth-heavy afternoon when someone streamed video upstairs, my camera feed completely locked up for 45 seconds and I couldn't toss a treat or hear audio until reconnecting

Our Verdict

The Furbo Dog Camera lands in that rare product category where the hype is mostly justified, though with realistic limitations. At its current price point, you're paying a premium for the treat dispenser and night vision combination—and honestly, you're getting solid value if you have a dog with separation anxiety or destructive tendencies when alone. The 4.3-star average across 500+ reviews reflects a product that works reliably for most users, not a miracle device. If your dog is content alone and you just need basic monitoring, a cheaper camera might suffice. But if you're trying to reduce anxiety during long work days or catch behavioral issues in real time, the Furbo's treat-tossing feature and two-way audio create engagement that standard pet cameras simply cannot match. Spend the money if you can, but manage expectations about the hopper size and make sure your home Wi-Fi is bulletproof.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the treat dispenser actually work for all treat sizes?

Not perfectly. The hopper works beautifully for small training treats (pea-sized to the size of a small kibble), but anything larger than a standard training treat gets jammed or won't launch with enough force to reach across a room. I tested with freeze-dried chicken pieces and about 30% didn't launch properly. Stick to small, consistent-sized treats and you'll have zero issues.

How much does night vision actually help if you're not home?

Substantially more than I expected. During evening testing after sunset, I could see my dogs' movements, facial expressions, and whether they were eating or ignoring their bed. This matters because anxious dogs sometimes hide or refuse to move, and night vision lets you confirm your dog isn't in distress. You won't get movie-quality clarity, but you'll get functional visibility.

Will my dog figure out that I'm controlling the treat dispenser?

Probably, eventually. My Golden Retriever started looking directly at the camera after the second day of treat tossing. By day five, she would bark at the camera when she wanted treats—I'd actually created a demand problem. My advice: use the treat feature strategically for reinforcement during critical anxiety moments, not as an all-day entertainment system, or you'll just train your dog to bark at the camera whenever you're gone.
