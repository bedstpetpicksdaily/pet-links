Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ The divider panel is genuinely thoughtful engineering. Unlike cheaper crates with flimsy inserts, this panel is sturdy and adjusts to create a smaller space without compromising structural integrity. Perfect for housebreaking puppies or training rescue dogs who need a confined area—it prevents them from using one corner as a bathroom while sleeping in another.
  • ✓ Ventilation design actually works. The Indigo uses a double-door system with adequate airflow that prevents the stuffiness you get in budget plastic kennels. Real owners report their dogs stay cooler during summer months, which matters when you're leaving them crated for reasonable periods.
  • ✓ Assembly is straightforward without being flimsy. Many crates in this price range either take forever to set up or feel wobbly once assembled. This one strikes a practical balance—tools aren't required, and the final structure feels solid enough for active large breeds without excessive wiggling or noise.
  • ✓ The plastic construction holds up to repetitive use. Heavy chewers will still damage it, but normal wear over years doesn't compromise the structure the way metal crates sometimes crack or rust. Easy to clean, resists staining, and doesn't harbor odors like fabric alternatives.
Cons
  • ✗ The divider panel creates a learning curve during installation. Instructions aren't crystal clear about optimal positioning, and the first time you adjust it, you might think something's wrong when it's actually working as designed. This isn't a deal-breaker, but it's worth knowing upfront.
  • ✗ Noise levels can be higher than metal crates for dogs who pace or move around frequently. The plastic panels resonate slightly with movement, which matters if the crate's in your bedroom or if you have a sensitive dog who gets anxious. Some owners report the sound is actually calming for their dogs—it depends entirely on the individual dog's temperament.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the divider panel stay put during normal use?

Yes, but with caveats. The divider locks into position securely enough for typical puppy movement and house-training scenarios. If you have a dog that launches themselves at the crate or applies extreme force (which you shouldn't be allowing anyway), you might need to check it periodically. Most owners report zero issues after the first week of use once they understand how the locking mechanism engages.

Is this crate too small for large breed dogs?

The Large size measures appropriately for dogs up to about 80 pounds, though the experience varies by individual dog size and shape. A 90-pound lab might feel cramped; a 75-pound lean dog will have comfortable movement. Measure your dog from nose to tail base and floor to head, then compare against listed dimensions rather than relying on breed alone. The divider, once removed, gives you the full crate space for adult dogs.

How does this compare to metal crates for durability?

Different categories of durability. Metal crates resist severe chewing but can rust, develop sharp edges, and sometimes bend when subjected to extreme force. The Petmate Indigo won't bend, won't rust, and won't develop sharp hazards—but sustained heavy chewing will eventually breach the plastic. For dogs that mouth gently or don't obsessively chew, the plastic longevity is comparable to metal crates. For determined chewers, metal is the safer bet despite other drawbacks.

Can you use the divider with the double-door setup?

Functionally yes, practically it depends on your space. The divider works with both doors available, but having two doors actually makes the divider system slightly less effective because a determined dog can navigate around it through either opening. Single-door functionality with the divider creates the most secure training environment. Plan your setup accordingly based on your specific needs.
