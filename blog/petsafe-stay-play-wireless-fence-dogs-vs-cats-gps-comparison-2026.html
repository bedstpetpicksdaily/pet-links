Frequently Asked Questions

What's the real difference between the GPS dog version and the standard wireless dog fence?

The GPS version adds real-time location tracking via mobile app (updates every 3-5 seconds, 5-foot radius accuracy) to the standard wireless range and correction system. This lets you actively track and intercept escapes in progress. The standard version relies solely on correction/warning beeps to train boundary respect. GPS costs more but transforms the system from passive containment to active tracking—critical for escape artists. The standard version works fine for dogs that already have moderate boundary respect.

Can the cat version work if my yard has trees, bushes, or underground utilities?

No, not reliably. Dense vegetation and buried metal structures (including underground utilities) interfere directly with the wireless signal, reducing coverage and creating dead zones. The cat version is light-duty by design, so signal degradation is more problematic than with the dog model. Before purchasing the cat system, verify your containment zone has a clear path from the staked transmitter with minimal vegetation and no buried utilities within the 100-foot range.

Does the dog's size matter, and will it work with all breeds?

Yes. The collar receiver must fit your dog properly to maintain reliable signal reception. Very large breeds and very small breeds may experience fit issues or inconsistent coverage with the standard collar. Verify your dog's weight and neck size against the manufacturer's specifications before purchasing. Additionally, wireless fences work better with dogs that have moderate to high food/praise motivation (they learn from corrections) and worse with independent, high-prey-drive dogs that ignore warnings under arousal.

If my dog leaves the boundary, what happens—do they get shocked immediately?

The system gives a warning beep first (audible alert to signal the dog is approaching the boundary), then applies adjustable-level stimulation if the dog continues into the correction zone. You control the stimulation intensity with adjustment levels (Level 1-2 is typically sufficient for smart dogs that respond to the warning). The system doesn't create an instant hard stop like a physical fence; it relies on the dog learning and respecting the boundary after repeated warning cycles.

How large of an area can the wireless transmitter actually cover?

The standard wireless range is 100 feet in open areas (no obstructions). In practice, signal range degrades with metal structures, dense vegetation, and underground utilities within that zone. You can define containment as circles or custom boundary shapes on the app (for GPS dog version), but the actual coverage depends on your yard's physical layout. If your containment area requires more than 100 feet of range or your yard has significant metal/vegetation interference, a wireless system will underperform.

Is this system safe for cats, and will the stimulation hurt them?

The cat version is calibrated for smaller, lighter animals, but the system is still based on electric stimulation, which carries inherent risk if the collar is too tight or malfunctions. Fit and comfort are critical—the collar must sit properly on your cat's neck to function safely. The bigger question isn't safety; it's effectiveness. Most cats won't be trained by corrections the way dogs are; they'll simply ignore the warning and leave the zone. Cats have far less motivation to respect an invisible boundary than dogs do.

Do I need WiFi or a phone plan for the GPS tracking to work?

The GPS dog version requires a mobile app to function, which means you need WiFi or cellular data on your phone to view real-time location updates and define boundaries. The receiver collar transmits to your phone via the app, so consistent connectivity improves tracking reliability. The standard (non-GPS) wireless fence does not require WiFi or app connectivity—it's purely collar-to-transmitter wireless signals.
