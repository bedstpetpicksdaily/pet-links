Why Summer Heat Is Dangerous for Dogs

Dogs regulate their body temperature differently than humans, primarily through panting and limited sweat glands. When temperatures soar, dogs face increased risks of heat exhaustion and heat stroke. During summer training sessions, it's crucial to recognize signs of overheating: excessive panting, drooling, lethargy, and red gums. By choosing appropriate training treats and lightweight gear, you can maintain your dog's routine while keeping them safe from heat-related complications.

Summer Training Best Practices

Beyond treats and collars, timing and hydration are essential. Schedule training sessions during early morning or late evening when temperatures are cooler. Always bring fresh water, take frequent breaks in shaded areas, and avoid hot pavement that can burn paw pads. Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use regular training treats during summer?

While you can, lightweight and natural options like Gnarly Pet's training treats are better for summer because they're less calorie-dense and won't contribute to overheating. They also require smaller portions, reducing your dog's heat intake.

What should I look for in a summer dog collar?

Look for lightweight materials, breathable fabrics, quick-dry capabilities, and reflective properties for safety. Gnarly Pet's collars are specifically designed with these features to keep your dog comfortable and visible during summer outings.

How often should I train my dog during summer?

Train during cooler parts of the day—early morning or late evening. Keep sessions shorter and include more frequent breaks with fresh water. Use high-value treats like Gnarly Pet's natural chews to maintain motivation without overtraining.

Are natural dog treats better for hot weather?

Yes, natural treats typically contain fewer fillers and additives, making them easier on your dog's digestive system and less likely to cause discomfort in heat. Gnarly Pet's protein-packed options are excellent summer choices.

How can I tell if my dog is overheating?

Signs include excessive panting, drooling, lethargy, red gums, and lack of coordination. If you notice these symptoms, move your dog to a cool area immediately, offer water, and contact your vet if symptoms persist.
